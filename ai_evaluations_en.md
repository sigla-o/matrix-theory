### 1.7. In-Depth Question: Interpretation of the Higgs 125 GeV Anomaly

This part of the conversation arose from the user's request to precisely describe how the 125 GeV value was obtained in the Standard Model and how Matrix Theory interprets it.

#### Standard Model View of 125 GeV (according to Gemini)

- **Theoretical basis:** In the Standard Model, the Higgs boson mass is not a fixed constant determined by the Universe's architecture. It depends on two quantities in the Higgs field potential formula: the vacuum expectation value *v* ≈ 246 GeV (known from muon decay) and the **free parameter λ** (the Higgs field self-coupling constant), which the theory cannot predict. Formula: m_H = √(2λ) · v.
- **Experimental acquisition:** Since λ could theoretically be anything, the LHC experiments provided the answer. In 2012, the ATLAS and CMS detectors registered a statistical "bump" (peak) at 125 GeV by analyzing collision products. The mass value was obtained by measuring the energy and angles of decay products (e.g., two photons or four leptons).
- **The "knife-edge" anomaly:** Interestingly, the 125 GeV value places our Universe on the boundary between absolute stability and instability (metastability). At this mass, our vacuum is currently stable, but at extreme energies, it has a tendency to "jump" to another state.
